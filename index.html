About Me

I am a political scientist and PhD researcher at the European University Institute. Broadly, my work examines how courts respond to social and political change, with a particular focus on gender-based violence, public opinion, and the legitimacy of the judiciary. I combine large-scale administrative data from the Spanish judiciary with causal inference methods and survey and conjoint experiments to study judicial behaviour and public trust in the justice system.

Before starting my PhD, I studied Political Science and Philosophy at the University of Barcelona and worked as a research assistant on several projects on gender inequality, judicial careers, and education policy. These experiences shaped my interest in how institutions can both reproduce and mitigate inequalities, and how citizens perceive the fairness of judicial outcomes.

Dissertation Projects

My dissertation investigates how judges make decisions in gender-based violence cases, and how public opinion, institutional reforms, and emerging technologies shape both judicial behaviour and public trust in the justice system.

  1. Judging Under Pressure: Public Opinion and Judicial Decisions in Sexual Violence Cases
    This project examines when and why judges respond to public opinion. Using approximately 10,000 rulings on sexual violence and theft from Spanish intermediate courts (2015–2020), I study how the highly publicised “Manada” case and its subsequent overturning by the Supreme Court affected conviction rates. I show that responsiveness is conditional: nationally, judges aligned with the Supreme Court rather than with protest demands, while locally, in Navarra, exposure to protests was associated with higher conviction rates in sexual violence cases.
  2. Algorithmic Justice and Gender-Based Violence: Fairness, Alignment, and Public Perception
    Co-authored with Siegfried Manschein.
    As judicial systems explore algorithmic tools to assist decision-making, concerns arise about fairness, transparency, and trust. In this project, we reconstruct gender-based violence cases from legal databases to fine-tune large language models and compare their suggested resolutions with actual judicial outcomes. We then run a conjoint experiment to examine how different process features – including algorithmic recommendations, chatbot assistance, risk assessments, and transparency – shape citizens’ perceptions of fairness in the judicial process.
  3. The Effects of Intimate Partner Violence Courts on Judicial Outcomes
    Co-authored with Joan-Josep Vallbé.
    Many civil law systems have introduced specialised courts for intimate partner violence, yet their impact on judicial performance remains understudied. Using administrative data from Spanish courts (2005–2023) and a staggered difference-in-differences design, this project exploits population thresholds and temporal variation in the creation of specialised courts to assess their effects on conviction rates, protection orders, and victim support measures.
