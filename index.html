About Me

I am a political scientist and PhD researcher at the European University Institute. My work examines how courts respond to social and political change, with a particular focus on gender-based violence, public opinion, and the legitimacy of the judiciary.

I combine administrative court data with causal inference and experimental methods to understand both judicial behaviour and citizens’ perceptions of fairness. Before the PhD, I worked as a research assistant on projects related to gender inequality, judicial careers, and education policy, and studied Political Science and Philosophy at the University of Barcelona.

Dissertation Projects

  1. Judging Under Pressure: Public Opinion and Judicial Decisions in Sexual Violence Cases
    Using ~10,000 rulings (2015–2020), I examine how the highly publicised “Manada” case shaped conviction rates in Spanish courts. I show that judges tended to align with the Supreme Court’s overturn rather than national protest demands, while local exposure to protests increased conviction rates in Navarra.
  2. Algorithmic Justice and Gender-Based Violence (with Siegfried Manschein)
    We reconstruct GBV cases using legal databases to fine-tune large language models and compare algorithmic resolutions with judicial decisions. A conjoint experiment evaluates how transparency, risk assessments, and AI assistance shape perceptions of fairness in judicial processes.
  3. The Effects of Intimate Partner Violence Courts (with Joan-Josep Vallbé)
    Using administrative data (2005–2023) and a staggered diff-in-diff design, we study how specialised IPV courts affect conviction rates, protection measures, and judicial performance across Spanish districts.
